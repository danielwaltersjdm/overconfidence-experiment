Introducing the Center for AI Forecasting and Calibration

Daniel Walters · April 6, 2026

Today we're launching the Center for AI Forecasting and Calibration and its flagship project: a live, public index that tracks whether frontier AI models are overconfident in their predictions.

The problem

When you ask an AI model to predict tomorrow's stock price or next week's weather, it doesn't just give you a number — it often gives you a confidence interval. "I'm 90% sure the S&P 500 will close between 5,200 and 5,400." That interval is a claim about the model's own uncertainty.

But is the model actually 90% sure? If you collected a hundred of those "90% confident" predictions, would 90 of them turn out right?

In human psychology, the answer is usually no. People routinely set their confidence intervals too narrow — a phenomenon called overconfidence. A person who says they're 90% sure is often right only 50–70% of the time. This has been one of the most robust findings in judgment and decision-making research for over fifty years.

We wanted to know: do AI models have the same problem?

What we found (so far)

Before launching this live tracker, we ran three cross-sectional studies testing Claude, GPT-4o, and Gemini on equity predictions, multi-horizon forecasts, and a multi-domain cross-section including crypto, forex, weather, and commodities. The results were striking:

  • GPT-4o is the most overconfident model in every study. Its stated 90% confidence intervals captured the true outcome only 46–71% of the time — far short of the 90% target.
  • Claude is the best calibrated overall, though still imperfect. Gemini falls in between.
  • All models show a domain-confidence inversion. They're overconfident in the domains where they seem most certain (equities, commodities) and underconfident in domains with higher volatility (crypto, forex).

But those were one-time snapshots. We couldn't tell whether the patterns would persist, improve, or worsen over time. That's why we built the live index.

How the live index works

Every weekday, an automated pipeline queries three frontier AI models — Claude, GPT-4o, and Gemini — with the same 40 prediction tasks across stocks, crypto, forex, and weather. Each model produces confidence intervals at the 50%, 80%, and 90% levels for three future horizons: 1 day, 1 week, and 1 month.

When the prediction horizon elapses, we fetch the actual outcome from public data sources and score it. The live dashboard shows a rolling 30-day average of 90% CI hit rates for each model.

If a model is perfectly calibrated, its 90% CI hit rate should be right around 90%. If it's significantly lower, the model is overconfident. It's that simple.

Why this matters

As AI systems are increasingly used for forecasting, planning, and decision support, the quality of their uncertainty estimates matters enormously. A financial analyst who relies on an AI's "90% confident" interval is implicitly trusting that the model knows the limits of its own knowledge. If it doesn't — if it systematically overstates its confidence — then decisions based on those intervals will be poorly calibrated too.

We think transparency about AI calibration is a public good. This index is our contribution.

What's next

The index is now collecting data daily. One-day predictions are already being scored; one-week data will become available in about a week, and one-month data in about a month. We'll publish updates here as patterns emerge and as we accumulate enough data to draw statistically meaningful conclusions.

All data and code are open source and available on GitHub.
